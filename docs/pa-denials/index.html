Public Assistance denials in NYC are at an all-time high during the pandemic

By Paroma Soni

Once the epicenter of the COVID-19 pandemic, New York City residents faced an incredibly challenging year. More than 300,000 people applied for public assistance (PA) – temporary cash aid meant for emergencies like a sudden change in income and shelter, inability to afford food, unexpected sickness and so on. The number of PA cases has surprisingly remained steady over the years, if not seen a little dip. In 2020, however, the number of denials were almost 50% – a 10% increase from last year and the highest they have ever been since the program began in 2006.

According to data from the OTDA (Office of Temporary and Disability Assistance), the top reason for case denials was a category known as “Financial Issues.” According to the data’s documentation, the reason for denial includes an “increased income, exceeded resource limits or other household circumstance changes that make the case ineligible for financial support.”

Essentially it means that people were earning too much – had more income or resources than the threshold, therefore making them ineligible for assistance.  “The resource limit for Public Assistance is $2,000 per month for a household. If the household contains a member who is disabled, or age 60 or over, the resource limit is $3,000,” said Anthony Farmer, an OTDA official who helped collect the data. “If a household has resources exceeding those amounts, the household is ineligible to receive assistance.”

In a pandemic year marked by severe unemployment, uncertainty and financial distress for households around the world, this data is surprising if not worrying. Fortunately, very few people were denied on the grounds of Employment compared to previous years. One of the probable reasons for that is that NY State made more that $1.7 million available for emergency unemployment relief, part of which was disbursed through the OTDA.

Under the Trump administration, many changes were made to the processes of applying for federal assistance. The process in New York State remained mostly unaffected, however, some parts of the PA program are dependent on national changes. For instance, the SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program), or food stamps, are frequently given as part of PA from the OTDA. The number of SNAP recipients increased dramatically during 2020, and so it is possible that PA counted SNAP as resources belonging to the household, explained Mr. Farmer.

Another possible explanation offered by Mr. Farmer is that people applied for PA without carefully examining the eligibility requirements. “Shortly after the start of the pandemic, many people quickly experienced unemployment and, unsure where to turn, many likely sought help anywhere they could, including applying for public assistance.”

This theory would hold if the number of applications increased, but that is not the case. The number of applications for PA was just over 311,000 in 2020, compared to the average 377,000 people per year in the last 15 years.
